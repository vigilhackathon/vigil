ABDOMINAL PAIN — ED NURSING ASSESSMENT

Abdominal pain is the most common chief complaint in adults >=65 presenting to the ED (1.4
million visits/year in the US), carries a 5.3% 60-day mortality in geriatric patients, and is
among the highest-risk conditions for mis-triage — with 8% mis-triage rate in large
retrospective series, where surgical emergencies are more often misdirected to medicine than
the reverse.

IMMEDIATE TRIAGE PRIORITIES (FIRST 60 SECONDS)

ESI Level I (Immediate resuscitation) — call physician NOW if any of:
- Hemodynamic instability: hypotension (SBP <90), tachycardia (HR >120), shock signs (pallor,
  diaphoresis, AMS, mottling)
- Pulsatile abdominal mass -> AAA until proven otherwise
- Peritoneal signs: rigid/board-like abdomen, guarding, rebound
- Syncope or near-syncope + abdominal pain
- Pregnancy + abdominal pain + hemodynamic instability -> ectopic rupture
- Severe pain score (>=9) + acute onset ("worst pain of life")

ESI Level II (Evaluation within 10 min) — urgent physician notification:
- Severe abdominal pain + fever >=38.5 C
- Signs of peritonitis without shock
- Immunocompromised patient with any abdominal pain
- Elderly patient (>65) with abdominal pain — always higher acuity than it appears
- Vomiting + distension + inability to pass stool/flatus -> obstruction
- Pain out of proportion to exam -> mesenteric ischemia or early NSTI

OMINOUS SIGNS — MUST NOT MISS
- Hemodynamic instability -> AAA rupture, ectopic, mesenteric ischemia, perforated viscus,
  sepsis. Immediate resuscitation, 2 large-bore IVs, physician NOW.
- Pulsatile epigastric/umbilical mass -> Ruptured AAA (mortality >80% without emergent
  surgery). Do not delay for CT if unstable — OR directly.
- Acute-onset maximal pain ("thunderclap") -> Ruptured AAA, aortic dissection, perforated
  viscus. Emergent CT angio if stable.
- Pain out of proportion to exam -> Mesenteric ischemia (classic triad; early stages
  deceptively benign). Emergent CT angiography; lactate.
- Rigid/board-like abdomen -> Perforated viscus, generalized peritonitis. NPO, IV access,
  surgical consult immediately.
- Positive Murphy's sign (RUQ pain + inspiratory arrest on palpation) -> Acute cholecystitis.
- McBurney's point tenderness + RLQ rebound -> Appendicitis.
- Bilateral lower extremity pain in elderly -> Mesenteric ischemia, not cellulitis.
- Pregnancy + unilateral adnexal pain/tenderness + hemodynamic instability -> Ectopic
  pregnancy — urine hCG first, always.
- Elderly patient with "mild" pain but tachycardia -> Never underestimate; atypical
  presentations are the rule. Full workup regardless of pain score.
- Fever + jaundice + RUQ pain (Charcot's triad) -> Acute cholangitis (add hypotension + AMS =
  Reynolds pentad -> septic shock).
- Distension + high-pitched or absent bowel sounds + obstipation -> Bowel obstruction.
- Flank pain radiating to groin + hematuria -> Urolithiasis. But ALWAYS rule out AAA first in
  >50yo.

STRUCTURED NURSING ASSESSMENT (OPQRST + CRITICAL HISTORY)

Pain characterization:
- Onset: Sudden = perforation, AAA rupture, ectopic; gradual = inflammatory (appendicitis,
  cholecystitis, PID).
- Location -> migration:
  - Periumbilical -> RLQ migration = appendicitis (LR+ 4.81 for pain migration).
  - Epigastric -> diffuse = perforation, pancreatitis.
  - RUQ = cholecystitis, hepatitis, cholangitis.
  - LLQ = diverticulitis (severe LLQ in elderly first).
  - Diffuse = peritonitis, mesenteric ischemia, early bowel obstruction.
  - Flank/loin -> groin = urolithiasis (but rule out AAA in >50yo).
  - Back + epigastric = pancreatitis, AAA.
- Quality: Colicky (waves) = hollow viscus (biliary colic, ureteral stone, obstruction);
  constant = inflammatory or ischemic.
- Radiation: Right shoulder = diaphragmatic irritation (free air, hepatic); back =
  pancreatitis, AAA; left shoulder (Kehr's sign) = splenic pathology, hemoperitoneum.
- Severity: High pain scores paradoxically increase correct triage accuracy.
- Timing: Postprandial (fatty foods) = biliary; nocturnal = peptic ulcer; post-alcohol binge
  = pancreatitis.

Critical associated symptoms:
- Vomiting: before pain -> gastroenteritis; after pain -> surgical.
- Last bowel movement / flatus: obstipation = obstruction until excluded.
- Hematochezia/melena: GI bleeding, ischemic colitis.
- Dysuria/hematuria: UTI, ureteral stone, but also appendicitis.
- Vaginal bleeding/discharge: ectopic, PID, miscarriage.
- Fever: absent in elderly/immunocompromised even with serious pathology — do NOT reassure
  yourself with a normal temperature.
- Anorexia/nausea: appendicitis, bowel obstruction.

High-risk history (ask every patient):
- LMP + pregnancy status in all women of childbearing age -> urine beta-hCG immediately.
- Menstrual/gynecologic history: ovarian torsion, endometriosis, ectopic.
- Prior surgeries (esp. abdominal) -> adhesions/obstruction; bariatric patients high-risk with
  deceptively benign exams.
- Cardiac history + atrial fibrillation -> mesenteric ischemia (embolic).
- AAA history or known aneurysm.
- Anticoagulants/steroids: mask peritoneal signs; steroids suppress fever.
- Immunocompromise (HIV, transplant, chemotherapy, diabetes) -> atypical presentations.
- IBD/prior similar episodes. Alcohol use. Travel/sick contacts.

KEY POPULATIONS
- Elderly (>65): most dangerous group — 40% experience admission, operation, or death within
  2 weeks. Atypical presentations are the norm (minimal pain, normal temperature, normal WBC
  all falsely reassuring). Default to higher acuity regardless of pain score or appearance.
- Women of childbearing age: beta-hCG immediately at triage, no exception. Ectopic can rupture
  at any beta-hCG level; stable vitals do not exclude active hemorrhage.
- Pregnant: appendicitis is the most common non-obstetric surgical emergency; pain location
  shifts cephalad; US first-line, MRI if equivocal (avoid CT).
- Bariatric surgery history: exam notoriously unreliable; any persistent pain + tachycardia +
  tachypnea -> low threshold for surgical consult. Do NOT be reassured by stable appearance.
- Immunocompromised: absent fever and near-normal labs despite serious pathology; low
  threshold for broad workup and early ID/surgical consult.

COMMON DIAGNOSES BY LOCATION
- RUQ: cholecystitis, cholangitis, hepatitis, peptic ulcer. Escalate if jaundice + fever + AMS
  (Reynolds pentad = septic shock).
- Epigastric: pancreatitis, peptic ulcer, GERD, MI, AAA. Escalate if lipase >3x normal or
  hemodynamic instability.
- RLQ: appendicitis, ovarian torsion, ectopic, Crohn's. Escalate if peritoneal signs, positive
  beta-hCG, high AIR/Alvarado.
- LLQ: diverticulitis, ovarian cyst/torsion, sigmoid volvulus. Escalate if severe pain + fever
  + mass (abscess/perforation).
- Diffuse: peritonitis, mesenteric ischemia, bowel obstruction, DKA. Board-like abdomen,
  distension, pain out of proportion.
- Flank/back: urolithiasis, pyelonephritis, AAA. >50yo + back pain -> rule out AAA first.
- Periumbilical: early appendicitis, bowel obstruction, mesenteric ischemia. Migrating RLQ +
  fever + nausea -> image emergently.

MIS-TRIAGE RISK (nursing-specific)
Retrospective study of 13,416 non-traumatic abdominal pain patients: 8.02% mis-triaged.
Independent risk factors: walking to triage (OR 1.76 — ambulatory surgical emergencies
frequently undertriaged), more accompanying symptoms (OR 1.22), lower pain scores (OR 0.78 per
point decrease — low pain scores paradoxically increase mis-triage risk), male sex (OR 1.14),
less experienced triage nurse.

CLINICAL RULE: Never let a low pain score, ambulatory arrival, or calm appearance falsely
reassure you. Elderly, bariatric surgery, immunocompromised, and pregnant patients require
higher default acuity regardless of how they look at triage. If uncertain, escalate — do not
reassess in an hour.
