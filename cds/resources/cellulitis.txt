OVERVIEW OF CELLULITIS

Definition & Pathophysiology
Cellulitis is a bacterial infection of the deep dermis and subcutaneous tissue, presenting
as diffuse, poorly demarcated (vs. sharp margins of erysipelas) expanding erythema, warmth,
tenderness, and edema — typically non-purulent. Erysipelas involves only the upper dermis
with sharp demarcation; in practice, management is identical.

Primary pathogens: beta-hemolytic streptococci (~70% of cases, particularly S. pyogenes and
group C/G streptococci including S. dysgalactiae subsp. equisimilis), S. aureus (MSSA > MRSA
in non-purulent disease).

Special host/site considerations:
- Cervical/facial: oral flora, anaerobes (Ludwig's angina)
- Lower extremities + diabetes/PVD: Enterobacteriaceae, anaerobes, Gram-negatives
- Immunosuppressed patients: Gram-negative organisms including Pseudomonas, E. coli, MDR
  pathogens — mortality 67% if Gram-negatives not covered
- Marine exposure + liver disease: Vibrio vulnificus (subtropical regions)

CRITICAL POINT: MISDIAGNOSIS RATE IS VERY HIGH
A systematic review and meta-analysis of 9 studies (n=1,600) found a mean misdiagnosis rate
of 41% (95% CI 28–56%), with stasis dermatitis, eczematous dermatitis, and edema/lymphedema
accounting for 54% of misdiagnoses. A dermatology RCT confirmed pseudocellulitis in 30.7% of
hospitalized patients with presumed cellulitis, directly altering management in 23.9%.
Bilateral lower extremity "cellulitis" is almost always a mimic.

Common pseudocellulitis mimics (ranked):
- Stasis/venous dermatitis (most common)
- Eczematous dermatitis
- Lymphedema
- Erythema migrans (Lyme disease)
- DVT
- Contact dermatitis
- Calciphylaxis (in ESRD patients)
- Pyoderma gangrenosum (progressive necrosis with debridement → suspect this)

ALT-70 score can aid differentiation: asymmetry (3 pts), leukocytosis WBC ≥10,000 (1 pt),
tachycardia HR ≥90 (1 pt), age ≥70 (2 pts) — score <3 = >83% likelihood of pseudocellulitis;
score >4 = >82% likelihood of true cellulitis.

DIAGNOSIS
Clinical diagnosis — no gold-standard test exists.
Do NOT routinely culture (blood or wound) in uncomplicated outpatient cellulitis; sensitivity
is low (<10% bacteremia in immunocompetent patients).
POCUS useful to exclude abscess when diagnostic uncertainty exists.
CT scan if necrotizing infection suspected (sensitivity 88.5%, specificity 93.3% for NSTI).

OMINOUS SIGNS — WHEN TO SUSPECT NECROTIZING FASCIITIS / NSTI
NSTI is initially misdiagnosed in ~71% of cases (systematic review, n=1,463), with mortality
up to 30% even with aggressive management. Delay beyond 12–24 hours to surgery dramatically
worsens outcomes; surgical intervention within 6 hours is associated with significantly
increased survival.

Sign / Feature — Clinical Significance
- Pain out of proportion to physical findings — hallmark early sign of NSTI, most critical
- Rapid progression despite appropriate antibiotics — failure to respond mandates surgical
  re-evaluation
- Hemorrhagic bullae — specificity 95.8% for NSTI, sensitivity 25.2%
- Crepitus (gas in tissues) — late sign; highly specific — found in ~20–25% of NSTI
- Skin necrosis / dusky / purple discoloration — late sign; indicates established fascial
  necrosis
- Focal anesthesia over the erythema — due to nerve destruction
- Hypotension / septic shock — specificity 97.7% for NSTI (sensitivity only 21%)
- Systemic toxicity (AMS, organ failure, high lactate) — mandates ICU-level care + immediate
  surgical consult
- "Dishwasher-fluid" pus at exploration — pathognomonic of NSTI intraoperatively

Key principle: Absence of any single finding does NOT rule out NSTI. A high clinical suspicion
warrants surgical consultation regardless of lab/imaging results.
LRINEC Score caveat: LRINEC ≥6 has sensitivity of only 68.2% and specificity 84.8% — it
cannot be used to rule out NSTI and does not replace clinical judgment; scores can remain 0–4
even in confirmed necrotizing fasciitis.

IDSA SEVERITY CLASSIFICATION & TREATMENT

Non-purulent Cellulitis
- Mild: No systemic signs; immunocompetent. Oral beta-lactam (cephalexin, amoxicillin,
  dicloxacillin) x 5–6 days.
- Moderate: Systemic signs (fever, tachycardia, hypotension, leukocytosis). IV therapy
  (cefazolin, ceftriaxone, nafcillin/oxacillin); transition to oral when improving.
- Severe: Failed oral therapy, immunocompromise, bullae, skin sloughing, hypotension, organ
  dysfunction. IV vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam empirically; add broader coverage if
  NSTI suspected.

Add MRSA coverage (TMP-SMX, doxycycline oral; vancomycin IV) when: penetrating trauma,
evidence of MRSA elsewhere/colonization, injection drug use, or SIRS.
Immunosuppressed patients: Start broad-spectrum empirically (cover Gram-positives +
Gram-negatives + anaerobes); de-escalate based on cultures within 24–48h.

Necrotizing Fasciitis (if escalated)
- Immediate surgical debridement — highest priority; return to OR every 24–48h until no
  necrosis.
- Empiric antibiotics: vancomycin or linezolid + piperacillin-tazobactam or carbapenem
  (± ceftriaxone + metronidazole).
- If GAS confirmed/suspected: Add clindamycin (antitoxin effect) + penicillin G; linezolid is
  non-inferior to clindamycin as antitoxin adjunct.
- Continue antibiotics until apyrexial ≥48–72h after last debridement.

WHEN TO ESCALATE — DECISION FRAMEWORK

Escalate to IV antibiotics / hospitalize if any of:
- Systemic signs (T >38°C, HR >90, RR >24, WBC >12,000 or <400)
- Failed oral antibiotics after 48–72h
- Immunocompromised host (transplant, HIV, neutropenia, DM with poor control)
- Rapidly expanding erythema despite antibiotics
- Involvement of face/orbit/genitalia
- Unable to tolerate oral medications

Call surgery IMMEDIATELY if any of:
- Pain out of proportion to exam
- Bullae / skin necrosis / crepitus
- Purple or bronze discoloration
- Failure to improve with broad IV antibiotics
- Septic shock or organ failure
- "Finger test" positive (gray necrotic fascia, no bleeding, no resistance to blunt
  dissection at bedside)

BLOOD CULTURES: SELECTIVE USE ONLY
- Uncomplicated immunocompetent cellulitis: Do NOT routinely culture.
- Immunocompromised: Obtain blood cultures.
- Intravascular prosthesis / pacemaker / valve: Obtain blood cultures (S. aureus bacteremia
  consequences).
- Suspected non-standard pathogen (marine/animal exposure): Obtain blood cultures.

NON-RESPONSE AT 48–96 HOURS
- Do not reflexively extend antibiotics — reassess the diagnosis first (pseudocellulitis?
  abscess? NSTI?).
- Consider dermatology or ID consult.
- Consider POCUS or CT to exclude abscess/deep infection.
- If truly worsening despite appropriate antibiotics → surgical exploration, not more
  antibiotics.
